Khan Bank Product Risk Rating Methodology

Each Khan Bank investment product is assigned a risk rating from 1 (Conservative) to 6 (Very Aggressive) using a transparent, multi-parameter weighted scoring model. The methodology is aligned with international best practices from MAS (Singapore), ESMA PRIIP, and leading private banks, adapted for the Mongolian market context.

Scoring Formula

Rating = f(Weighted Score)

Raw Score =
Capital at Risk × 30% +
Market Volatility × 25% +
Credit Risk × 20% +
Liquidity Risk × 15% +
Product Complexity × 5% +
Investment Horizon × 5%

Final Rating = ceil(Raw) if decimal ≥ 0.15
floor(Raw) if decimal < 0.15
Leverage override: Final Rating = 6

The engine rounds conservatively. Any product whose weighted score exceeds a whole number by more than 0.15 is placed in the higher rating band, so borderline products fall on the more cautious side. Leverage is an unconditional override: any leveraged product is automatically Rating 6 regardless of other parameters.

Override Rules

  • Leverage / Margin: Any product with borrowing or margin trading is automatically Rating 6, regardless of other parameter scores. No exceptions.
  • Unrated / Distressed Issuers: For bonds from unrated or CCC-rated issuers, minimum Credit Risk = 5. Final rating cannot be lower than 4.
  • Annual Review: All product ratings must be reviewed at minimum annually, or immediately upon material change in issuer credit quality, market conditions, or product structure.
  • New Products: Any product not listed in the approved library must be rated by the Product Risk Committee before client distribution.

Rating Conventions

  • National Scale Credit: Credit quality of domestic MNT obligations is assessed on a national scale through Khan Bank's internal borrower grade, which measures local currency repayment capacity. The hard currency sovereign ceiling applies to foreign currency instruments only. This mirrors the national scale rating practice of the international agencies, which exists precisely because the global scale compresses all frontier market issuers into a narrow band.
  • Base Currency: Ratings are expressed from the perspective of an MNT based investor. Foreign currency instruments carry FX risk in their volatility and capital scores. This is why the sovereign's USD eurobond rates above the same sovereign's domestic MNT bond, and why a short MNT T-bill can carry a low rating.

Corporate Credit Methodology for Unrated Issuers

Most Mongolian bonds and issuers are not rated by Fitch, Moody's or S&P. The engine therefore uses Khan Bank's own corporate-credit methodology as the internal substitute for an external rating. The bank grades every borrower on a 1 to 10 scale (industry and business risk, management risk, and financial risk including the Altman Z-score) and every facility on an A to F scale (use of proceeds, tenor, currency, repayment structure, collateral and cash-flow coverage), producing a composite grade such as 4A.

The borrower grade maps to the engine's six-point credit parameter as follows: grade 1 maps to credit 2; grades 2 to 3 map to credit 3 (investment-grade equivalent); grades 4 to 5 map to credit 4; grades 6 to 7 map to credit 5; grades 8 to 10 map to credit 6. The facility grade then adjusts the result by one notch: strong structures (A to B) improve the credit score by one, weak structures (E to F) worsen it by one, standard structures (C to D) leave it unchanged.

Where the bank has not yet assigned an internal grade and no agency rating exists, the issuer defaults to credit 5 and the engine's unrated override holds the final product rating at no lower than 4. The effect is deliberate: a genuinely strong but agency-unrated Mongolian issuer, such as a listed bank or a top-20 corporate, can be recognised at investment-grade-equivalent credit through the bank's own grade, while only the truly ungraded are treated as speculative.

Expanded Mongolian Asset-Class Taxonomy

The product library now reflects the structure of the Mongolian and cross-border markets. Listed equities are classified by Mongolian Stock Exchange board: Board 1 (the largest, highest-quality issuers), Board 2 (mid-tier), and Board 3 (speculative issuers including start-ups). Government bonds distinguish the sovereign from municipal issuers, and domestic local-currency issuance from hard-currency eurobonds. Corporate bonds are classified by venue (domestic exchange, domestic over-the-counter, and eurobond) and by issuer tier (top 20, top 100, and outside the top 100), because venue drives liquidity and issuer tier drives credit. The taxonomy also covers asset-backed securities issued by non-bank financial institutions against their loan portfolios, typically listed on the exchange; these carry a complex product flag requiring an appropriateness assessment before sale. Physical real estate (residential and commercial) is rated for reference in a separate alternatives annex, pending a Product Risk Committee decision on whether direct assets belong in a securities distribution library. Named MSE instruments appear as worked examples in Annex A; standing instrument ratings are assigned through the engine at onboarding. Selected foreign instruments are included so the scale is calibrated against global reference points such as US Treasuries, developed-market equities and gold.

A broader quality screen, "top 100 in the country", sits alongside the board classification. It sets the credit assumption for an ungraded issuer and corroborates the board placement. The screen has limited effect on an equity rating, where capital at risk and volatility dominate, but a material effect on that issuer's bonds, where credit quality carries more weight.

Production adoption of the full PRIIPs Annex II computation — the 97.5% VaR equivalent volatility on observed return series, the credit quality step based credit risk measure, and external validation of the national scale credit mapping — is a defined Phase 2B deliverable.
